## Appendix C — Soft Skills: Leadership Philosophy & Interview Prep

### Leadership Philosophy

My approach to leadership comes from five years of managing volunteer teams — people who can walk away at any moment. That constraint made me a better leader than any management training would have.

The core insight: **everyone is a volunteer.** Employees are paid, but they're constantly deciding whether to go above and beyond or just coast. The job of a good leader is to make going above and beyond feel worthwhile — through meaningful work, genuine recognition, creative freedom, and the sense that someone is paying attention to them as an individual.

What I've learned:
- **1:1s are the most powerful tool I have.** Volunteers (and employees) say things in a 1:1 that they'll never say in a group. They'll surface blockers, frustrations, and ambitions that would otherwise fester. The personalized attention signals that they matter.
- **Manage with a light touch.** Stay technically informed enough to have context on the work, but don't micromanage. The goal is to remove blockers, not create them.
- **Give real creative freedom, then trust it.** The tools I built for my artist at Star Garden Games weren't just about efficiency — they were about giving him genuine ownership of his domain. That ownership produced better art and a stronger working relationship.
- **Help people find their fit, not just their task.** When a volunteer's work isn't the right fit, the earlier you address it with honesty and care, the better the outcome for everyone. A 1:1 is the right place for that conversation.

### Mock Interview Q&A

**Q: Tell me about a time you motivated someone without formal authority.**

*Strong answer:* At Equal Vote, all my direct reports were volunteers — they had no financial stake in staying. I kept people engaged through a combination of clear purpose (they believed in voting reform), meaningful work (not just grunt tasks), and personal investment. I ran regular 1:1s where I'd check in on how the work was sitting with them, not just whether tasks were done. When someone was losing motivation, I'd usually find out in a 1:1 first — before it showed up in their output — and could adjust their role or responsibilities before they disengaged.

---

**Q: How do you handle underperformance on a team where you can't use traditional accountability?**

*Strong answer:* Honestly, the lack of traditional accountability forced me to get better at root causes. When a volunteer was underperforming, it was almost never laziness — it was usually a mismatch: wrong task, unclear expectations, or a personal situation I didn't know about. I'd address it in a 1:1, start by asking how things were going, and listen before diagnosing. If the work genuinely wasn't a fit, I'd help them find a different role on the project or a graceful exit. Preserving the relationship mattered — both because it was the right thing to do and because word travels in small communities.

---

**Q: How do you balance giving people creative freedom with maintaining quality and direction?**

*Strong answer:* I try to set clear constraints on what matters most (the outcome, the deadline, the non-negotiables) and then genuinely let go of the how. At Star Garden Games I built tooling specifically to give my artist full independence over visual decisions — particles, animations, sounds — without needing my sign-off. That wasn't just efficient; it made the art better because he could experiment freely. For engineering volunteers, I invested heavily in documentation and onboarding so people could work independently rather than waiting on me for every decision.

**Q: How do you keep distributed or async teams aligned without micromanaging?**

*Strong answer:* Meeting structure was one of the most important things I got right at Equal Vote. I rotated between work sessions (just getting things done together), agendized meetings (high-level decisions), and standups (quick alignment). The goal was to make attendance feel like a good use of time — because if volunteers didn't see value in showing up, they'd stop. I also invested in documentation so that alignment didn't depend on everyone being in the same room at the same time. The goal was a team that could function without me, not one that needed me in every loop.
